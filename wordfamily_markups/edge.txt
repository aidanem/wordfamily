# The modern English meaning of `eager`=eng-Latn is a local innovation. English also kept "sharp, sour" as a secondary meaning until 19thC; that's what Shakespeare usually means by `eager`=eng-Latn, e.g., "It is a nipping and an eager (sharp) air" (Hamlet 1.4.2) or "And curd, like eager (sour) droppings into milk" (Hamlet 1.5.69).

## The word `ear`=eng-Latn, as in an ear of corn, and `ear`=eng-Latn, as in the part of your body you hear with are unrelated, from PIE roots <*h₂eḱ-> and <*h₂ows->, respectively.

The word `hear`=eng-Latn is from a compound of both of those roots: <*h₂ḱh₂owsyéti> from <*h₂eḱ-> (sharp) + <*h₂ows-> (ear) + <*-yéti> (intransitive imperfective verb ending), "to be sharp-earing"

### I really wanted Emilian `gùccia`=egl-Latn: "sewing needle" to be the source of Guccio Gucci's names, as an occupational surname for a tailor. But it's not, `Guccio`=ita-Latn is a version of `Hugo`=eng-Latn (ultimately from West Germanic <*hugi>)

#### Classical Greek `ἀξῑ́νη`=grc-Grek <axī́nē> is derivable either from Proto-Indo-European, or from a Semitic borrowing. Compare Akkadian `𒍏𒄩𒍣𒅔`=akk-Xsux <ḫaṣṣinnu>: "axe" and Aramaic `𐡇𐡑𐡉𐡍𐡀`=arc-Armi <ḥaṣīnā>: "axe" from Semitic <*ḥaṣṣ->. Latin `ascia`=lat-Latn and Germanic <*agʷésih₂> could also be from the Semitic borrowing, but the evidence is more towards the PIE root for those.

##### <*ḱeh₃-> looks like metathesis from <*h₂eḱ-> and has the same root meaning, but the <o> in Greek and <o>/<a> alternation in Latin require <*ḱeh₃->, not <*ḱeh₂->.


###### Latin `catus`=lat-Latn: "clever, cunning, sly" makes Linaeus' binomial name `Felis catus` (instead of `Felis cattus`) for the domestic cat, an (unintentional?) pun.


####### There is apparently an Iberian substrate word of some kind here behind Basque `unxti`=eus-Latn and Greek <kóniklos>, meaning either "rabbit" or "rabbit burrow" (unclear which way the semantic shift is going). There are a lot of possibilities, but I think the best single option is a Celtiberian word from <*ḱoh₃nos>: "sharp thing".

Borrowing of this word from an Iberian substrate is logical given the native range of the European rabbit (`Oryctolagus cuniculus`): Iberia, western France, and a bit of northwest Africa. Similarly, the Old French descendant `connin`=fro-Latn was widely borrowed in a later wave.

Compare that "Spain"/"Hispania" is from traditionally thought to be from Phoenician `𐤀𐤉 𐤔𐤐𐤍`=phn-Phnx <‘i špn>: "island of hyraxes/rabbits". Rabbits were a notable feature of Iberia to ancient visitors! Though that may be a very old folk etymology, <‘i ṣpn>: "northern/remote/hidden island", <‘i spn> "island of smithing" or <‘i spn> "island of sailors" are all plausible alternatives both phonologically and semantically.

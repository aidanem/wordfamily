# Avestan `𐬬𐬍𐬎𐬎𐬍𐬯𐬈`=ave-Avst <vīuuīse> is in the The Vendidad, chapter 2, paragraph 4. But I'm not sure what it means, "Make use"?

## If Old Norse `víking`=non-Latn is from Old Norse `vík`=non-Latn: "bay, cove, inlet", then it is effectively "bay-man". This is one of several possible origins for `viking`=eng-Latn. Eldar Heide has recently made some good arguments for it coming from a word related to "week" (<*weyk-> instead of <*weyḱ->), meaning "to take turns, for one thing to follow another"; in this case, a long sea voyage requiring multiple shifts of rowers.


### `York`=eng-Latn ultimately comes from  Brythonic <*Eburākon>: "Yew-Place", which is still represented by Welsh and Irish names from the place: `Efrog`=cym-Latn and `Eabhrac`=gle-Latn, respectively.

<*Eburākon> was Latinised as `Eburacum`=let-Latn. Then `Ebura-`=lat-Latn re-analyzed as Germanic <*eburaz>: "boar" -> Old English `Eofor-`=ang-Latn and `-cum`=lat-Latn translated as `-wic`=ang-Latn to give Old English `Eoforwic`=ang-Latn, still represented by Norman `Éverouic`=nrf-Latn.

Then `Eoforwic`=ang-Latn was adapted to Old Norse `Jórvík`=non-Latn after York was conquered by Ivar "the Boneless" Ragnarsson. In a later variant, the Old Norse was shortened to `Jórk`=non-Latn, and finally this variant was borrowed back into Middle English as `York`=enm-Latn.

#### The sandwich is named after John Montagu, Fourth Earl of Sandwich, so that he could eat without leaving either the gambling table or his work desk, depending on whether you believe his critics or his supporters.

The Sandwich Islands were also, but separately, named after John Montagu who, as First Lord of the Admiralty, granted the funding for Captain James Cook's expedition.

##### As far as I can tell, Albanian `zonjë e mirë`=sqi-Latn: "smallpox" literally means "The Good Lady". Possibly I'm misinterpreting that. If I'm not, I assume it is a euphemism designed not to anger Old King Plague. Compare "Eumenidies" ("the Kindly Ones") for the Furies, or "the Good Folk" for fairies.

###### Despite the fact that Lithuanian `viešė́ti`=lat-Latn means "to visit" and also looks an awful lot like "visit", Latinate English `visit`=eng-Latn is unrelated, as is Russian `на-вести́ть`=rus-Cyrl <na-vestítʹ>: "to visit" for that matter. Both of those are from [wff-veda|<*weyd->: "to see"] instead.

####### Japanese `バイキング(料理)`=jpn-Kana <Baikingu (ryōri)>: "smorgasbord, buffet", literally "Viking (cuisine)". The idea of the smorgasbord was imported to Japan from Sweden in the late 1950's, when the Kirk Douglas film "The Vikings" was also very popular in Japan. `Viking`=eng-Latn was much easier to adapt to a catchy Japanese word than `smörgåsbord`=swe-Latn!

In a fun coincidence, this ties "sandwich" and "viking" back together by a different route, as Swedish `smörgåsbord`=swe-Latn means "sandwich-table" (or "butter-goose-table", or "smear-goose-board").

# There is also quite a few forms descended from this re-analysis with /l/s at the end of the root that are not easily explained, possibly an additional part of the original re-analysis, or a subsequent re-analysis. Actually, there's a fairly compelling argument to made that all the forms with /l/ are secondarily derived from <*dʰeh₁léh₂>. In which case there's only one /l/ I need to explain.

## The languages that use this word for "son" lack both the standard IE words for <*suHnús> ("son") and <*dʰugh₂tḗr> ("daughter"), despite closely related langauges having the original words (e.g. Lithuanian does but Latvian doesn't; Oscan does but Latin doesn't). The modern Celtic languages also lack both <*suHnús> and <*dʰugh₂tḗr>, despite Gaulish having them (but use a different euphemisms from Latvian and Latin).

This probably represents taboo-avoidance: avoiding using the "real" word for "son" or "daughter" in order to avoid attracting the attention of malevolent spirits to your children.

### Besides the name of the Greek sea goddess, also thence the name of a prehistoric ocean, roughly consisting of the Mediterranean Sea plus the Indian Ocean before the Arabian peninsula split from Africa and divided it in half. Among other interesting things, this is the ocean in which the early whales transitioned back into the water, which is why early whale fossils are found in places like Egypt and Pakistan. One of my two favorite oceans that no longer exist.


#### Aphrodite and Helen both get this epithet in Homer, which is the exact cognate as Ushas epithet in the Rig Veda. This is one point among several suggesting that Aphrodite and Helen are both reflections of the [Proto-Indo-European Dawn Goddess, Hausos|easter].

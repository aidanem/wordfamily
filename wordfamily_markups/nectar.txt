## Old Persian `𐎻𐎡𐎴𐎠𐎰𐎹𐎫𐎡𐎹` <vi-i-n-a-θ-y-t-i-y>: "does harm" is in Darius' tomb inscription, in the line translated as  "The man who cooperates, him according to his cooperative action, him thus do I reward. Who does harm, him according to the damage thus I punish. It is not my desire that a man should do harm; nor indeed is that my desire, if he should do harm, he should not be punished." http://www.avesta.org/op/op.htm#dnb
http://titus.fkidg1.uni-frankfurt.de/database/titusinx/titusinx.asp?LXLANG=59288&LXWORD=vin0101iD103ayatiy&LCPL=0&TCPL=0&C=H&PF=14

#### Nectar, the drink of the gods, equivalent to <necro->+<trans->, "overcoming death". Compare Ambrosia, the food of the gods, equivalent to <a->+<mortos>, "without death".

<νέκταρ> (néktar) seems to have already been unanalyzable by a native speaker by Classical Greek. The underlying meaning was lost until 1952 when Paul Thieme correlated it with Vedic Sanskrit <tar->, "overcome", confirmed in 1961 when Rüdiger Schmitt realized it had a phrasal parallel in the Atharvaveda: <mrtyum tar-> "overcome death". (_How to Slay a Dragon_, Watkins 1995, p. 12)

# Latin `germānus`=lat-Latn: "sibling" and `Germānus`=lat-Latn: "German" are unrelated. `Germānus`=lat-Latn was probably borrowed from Gaulish. It has been interpreted as having meant either "neighbor folk" or "noisy folk".

## Hindi `उदजन`=hin-Deva <udjan> and English `hydrogen`=eng-Latn aren't just parallel constructs based on learned borrowings (Classical Sanskrit and Classical Greek, respectively), they are also formed by compounding the exact same two PIE roots: [WFF-water|<*wed-> (water)] + <*ǵenh₁-> (birth).


### Not the same as the other Welsh name `Morgan`=cym-Latn (literally "sea-circle", figuratively "sea-defender" or "sea-chief"). But Morgan le Fay first appears as `Morgen`=cym-Latn in Geoffrey of Monmouth's "The Life of Merlin".

The Irish `Muirgein`=gle-Latn especially refers to the mermaid-saint also known as Lí Ban. (Also, I'd just like to repeat the phrase, "the Irish mermaid-saint"!)


#### The name `Innogen`=eng-Latn first appears as the name of the King Pandrasus's daughter in the medieval British legendary history, "Brutus of Troy". Probably in an earlier form of the legend, she was identified only as the daughter of Pandrasus, and a later version then used the word meaning "daughter" as her proper name.

The variant `Imogen`=eng-Latn first appears in Shakespeare's _Cymbeline_. It it possible the variant already existed, but it is also very possible that Shakespeare wrote `Innogen`=eng-Latn but it was typeset as `Imogen`=eng-Latn instead. Shakespeare also used the name `Innogen`=eng-Latn in early drafts of _Much Ado About Noting_ about 12 years earlier; adding weight to the possibility that he intended to use `Innogen`=eng-Latn, not `Imogen`=eng-Latn in _Cymbeline_.

So, the English name `Imogen`=eng-Latn is ultimately based on two compounded literary mistakes (probably).


##### The military rank `general`=eng-Latn is a shortening of earlier `captain-general`=eng-Latn, i.e., the officer in charge of the army as a whole, the commander-in-chief; parallel to the surviving constructions `surgeon-general`=eng-Latn or `postmaster-general`=eng-Latn.


#######: Slavic <*kъnędzь>: "chief, prince, petty king" is from some early Germanic language. My preferred theory is Old Norse via the Rurikids and the Rus'.


######## `génie`=fra-Latn was used in French to translate the Arabic `جِنّ`=arb-Arab <jinn>, based on the similar sound and the meaning in Latin of "guardian spirit".

######### The meaning of `gentilis`=lat-Latn as "non-Jew" is a semantic loan from Hebrew `גוי`=heb-Hebr <goy>, literally "nation", but often used to mean "a nation other than Israel" and later "a non-Jew".

########## There is [https://papyrus-stories.com/2020/07/20/on-a-document-signed-by-cleopatra/|a document with the subscript `γινέσθωι`=grc-Grek: "make it happen"] (middle voice 3S imperative), perhaps written by the hand of Cleopatra. It orders tax exemptions to a roman, possibly Publius Canidius Crassus a lieutenant of Marcus Antonius (who appears in Shakespeare's _Antony and Cleopatra_).

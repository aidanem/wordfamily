There's a very cool fact, that cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower, kale, Brussels sprouts, collard greens, kohlrabi, and others are all the species of plant: Brassica oleracea, selectively bred for different parts of the plant.
https://www.google.com/search?q=brassica+oleracea+selection

I had known that for many years before I noticed that many of the English words are all the same word, too: cauliflower, kale, collard, kohlrabi, coleslaw.

But conspicously not:
`broccoli`=eng-Latn, despite the `col`=eng-Latn in the middle. `broccoli`=ita-Latn is a plural dimunitive of `brocco`=ita-Latn, just `cannoli`=ita-Latn is a plural dimunitive of [wff-cane|`canna`=ita-Latn: "tube, cane"]. `brocco`=ita-Latn probably means "pointy", related to "brooch" and "brocade".
`kailan`=eng-Latn/`gailan`=eng-Latn (or "Chinese kale") which, despite the 'kail' sequence, is really kai (or gai) + lan, and ultimately from Old Chinese <*kreːds> + <*ɡ·raːn>
`cabbage`=eng-Latn, of unknown origin, which is not explainable as some weird French version of `caulis`=lat-Latn. Though it may be a weird French versions of `caput`=lat-Latn: "head".
`Brussels sprouts`=eng-Latn, which of course is named after the city of Brussels. But in Romance languages, the term for "Brussels sprouts" is generally literally "cabbage of Brussels", and uses the Romance "cabbage" words in this family (e.g., `chou de Bruxelles`=fra-Latn, `cavolino di Bruxelles`=ita-Latn, `col de Bruselas`=spa-Latn); and in many Germanic languages is literally "rose-cabbage", and likewise uses their version of this word, e.g. `rosenkål=nor-Latn`, `Rosenkohl`=deu-Latn, etc.



<*ḱelh₂->, <*keh₂wl->, and <*h₂ewl-> all meaning "stem, stalk, reed, tube". This looks similar to the way certain very early PIE compounds have been simplified in a few divergent ways; perhaps it could be a pre-PIE compound that likewise diverged, something like <*kelH->: "to grow, to rise" + <*wel(H)->: "to turn, to wind".

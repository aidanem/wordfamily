startintro

Here I'm following Blažek's compelling argument (2017) to derive <*h₂ŕ̥Tḱos> from <*h₂r̥dh₂éḱh₃-s>: "bee-eater", similar to many later words for bear that mean things like "honey eater", "honey pig", "thief of bees", "bee bear", "bee wolf", … . This instead of the traditional connection to Sanskrit `𑀭𑀓𑁆𑀱𑀲𑁆`=san-Brah <rákṣas> and Avestan `𐬭𐬀𐬱𐬀𐬵`=ave-Avst <rašah>, both "harm, destruction".

Per Blažek, <*h₂r̥dh₂éḱh₃-s> gets to <*h₂ŕ̥dḱos> by a series of common changes: levelling of the inflection paradigm from a hysterokinetic genitive *h₂r̥dh₂ḱh₃-ós, plus regular absorption of the *h₃ by the *o and regular CHCC > CCC deleting the middle *h₂ (see also in <*h₂eḱneti>)

## The Noric name `𐌀𐌓𐌕𐌄𐌁𐌖𐌈𐌆`=nrc-Latn <Artebudz> has a disputed second element. It may be "Bear-Victory", "Bear-Lips", or "Bear-Penis". 


### The name Artemis (Classical Greek `Ἄρτεμις`=grc-Grek <Ártemis>, Mycenaean Greek `𐀀𐀳𐀖𐀵`=gmy-Linb <a-te-mi-to>) is of unknown origin, but given how often bears show up in the mythology and cultic practices around Artemis suggests there is some kind of connection to `ᾰ̓́ρκτος` <árktos>, either original or by folk etymology. 



#### There are many possible sources of the name `Arthur`=eng-Latn, almost all of them derive from <*h₂ŕ̥dk̑os>: "bear"

It could derive natively from Brythonic, in which case it is probably from Celtic <*Artorīxs>: "Bear-King".

Or it could be from Latin, as there is no evidence of anyone named "Arthur" until the 5th century at the earliest, shortly after the end of the Roman occupation of Brittain. If it is Latin, it could be from the Latin family name `Artorius`=lat-Latn (of indeterminate origin and meaning) which would become `Art(h)ur`=owl-Latn when borrowed into Welsh. Latin `Artorius`=lat-Latn may well derive from the patronymic of the equivalent Gaulish "Bear-King" name, <*Arto-rīg-ios>: "(son) of (the) Bear-King".

Finally, it could be from Latin `Arctūrus`=lat-Latn, borrowed from Greek "Bear-Watcher, Bear-Guard".

The folk etymology of Welsh `Arth-(g)wr`=cym-Latn (Brythonic <*arθ(o)gwur>): "bear-man" is almost certainly wrong. There is a fair amount of evidence from Welsh poetry that the name always rhymed with `-ur`=cym-Latn and not `-wr`=cym-Latn.

##### `Ἀρτεμῑ́σῐος`=grc-Grek <Artemī́sios> was a month name in some Classical Greek calendars, including the Corinthian and Macedonian calendars. The Corinthian month names, including <Artemī́sios>, are inscribed on the Antikythera mechanism


###### `Ursari`=rom-Latn or `Vursari`=rom-Latn are an ethnic group of Romani, descended from enslaved Romani in Wallachia (now Romania, no relation to `Romani`=eng-Latn). The Usari was among the groups allowed to retain their nomadic lifestyle, and usually worked as travelling performers, including with dancing bears, thus the name borrowed from Romanian `ursar`=ron-Latn: "bear tamer".

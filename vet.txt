# Latin "vitul(us/a" means a young cow, originally a one-year old cow. The Oscan equivalent (in the plural and/or genitive) ğŒ…ğŒğŒ•ğŒ„ğŒ‹ğŒ‰ğŒ` <vÃ­teliÃº> is thought to be the origin of Greek á¼¸Ï„Î±Î»Î¯Î± <ÄªtalÃ­Ä>: "Italy"

Latin "vitula" also means a kind of stringed instrument (and becomes "viula" in Occitan, whence "viola" and "violin"). This is clearly related to Germanic *fiÃ¾ulÇ­: "a stringed instrument" ("fiddle"), but it is unclear if "vitula": "stringed instrument" is from "vitula": "young cow" (compare the story of Hermes and the lyre) and Germanic borrowed it; or if the Germanic is of unknown origin and Latin borrowed it.

"vitula": "stringed instrument" is attested late enough that Latin could have borrowed it, and Germanic *Ã¾ borrowed as Latin 't' is more common than the reverse; so on whole it is probably most likely that  Late Latin borrowed the meaning on to the existing Latin word `vitula`: "a heifer calf" and/or `Vitula`: "divinity of Joy and Victory" (whose name may or may not be from `vitula`: "a heifer calf" and whose worshipped involved music).

## Hilariously, Latin `vernum`: "of spring, vernal" and Latin `hÄ«bernum`: "of winter" have both become `verno` in Italian.

### I always assumed "vellum": "calf or lamb skin for writing" was related to "vellus": "wool, the pelt or hide of an animal", but while "vellum" looks like a direct Latin word, but is actually from French "velin" from Latin "vitulinus": "of calf". Possibly some other Latin-knowing English speaker made the same assumption in the past and re-modeled "velin" to look like `vellus`.

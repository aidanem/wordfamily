# In the Middle English period `no` was originally a reduction of `none` in front of a consonant, just as `a` was a reduction of `an` in front of a consonant.


## Old English `nāwiht`, which gives both `not` and `nought` in modern English is `ne-` + `āwiht`. `āwight`: "anything" gives "ought" in modern English is itself `ā-`: "ever" + `wiht`: "wight, person, thing". So the apparently simple word "not" comes from "not ever a thing": <n>+<o>+<t>. Equivalent to Proto-Indo-European <*ne-h₂óyu-wektis>
